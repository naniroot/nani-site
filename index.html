What I'm working on

  • 📈 Kalshi Bot — 15-minute bitcoin predictions

    A trading bot that lives on Kalshi and does exactly one thing: predict whether bitcoin will be up or down at the close of each 15-minute window, then place the bet. Small stakes, fast feedback, endless tuning.

    The appeal of 15-minute markets is the loop speed. Instead of waiting days to learn whether a thesis held, I get a verdict every quarter hour — which means the bot generates a mountain of labeled data fast, and I can iterate on features without dying of boredom. It pulls short-horizon price action, order-book imbalance, and a few volatility signals, then outputs a calibrated probability for the next close.

    The hard part isn't the prediction, it's the discipline around it. Fees and the bid-ask spread quietly eat naive strategies alive, so most of the work has gone into position sizing and knowing when not to trade — the bot sits on its hands unless its edge clears a threshold. I treat calibration as the real scoreboard: being right 58% of the time is worthless if I thought it was 80%.

    Right now it's paper-trading against live markets while I watch how the edge decays and whether it survives contact with real slippage. Next up is a proper backtest harness and a kill-switch that pulls it offline the moment its calibration drifts. It's equal parts trading experiment and an excuse to get serious about probabilistic forecasting.

  • 🎮 Story games — with a touch of strategy

    My favorite way to switch off: story-driven worlds with just enough strategy to keep my brain engaged — think Breath of the Wild, The Last of Us, and Subnautica.

    What hooks me is the blend of narrative and systems. Breath of the Wild is the purest version of it — a world that hands you a physics sandbox and trusts you to be clever, where half the fun is solving a shrine in a way the designers clearly didn't plan for. I'll happily lose an evening to a single "what if I just…" experiment.

    The Last of Us is the other pole: tightly authored, emotionally heavy, the kind of story that stays with me long after the credits. And Subnautica scratches a different itch entirely — that slow-burn loop of fear and mastery as an alien ocean goes from terrifying to home base, one careful expedition at a time.

    The common thread is agency inside a world worth caring about. I'm less interested in twitch reflexes than in games that reward planning, curiosity, and the occasional reckless gamble. If you have a story-rich recommendation with a strategic streak, I'm all ears — say hi above.

  • 🧠 Growth mindset — the Carol Dweck kind

    An idea I keep coming back to: Dr. Carol S. Dweck's research on the growth mindset — the belief that ability is built through effort, not fixed at birth — and trying to actually live by it.

    Dweck's core distinction is deceptively simple. A fixed mindset treats talent as a static thing you either have or don't, so every challenge becomes a referendum on whether you're good enough. A growth mindset treats ability as something you develop, which turns the same challenge into information — a place to get better rather than a verdict on your worth.

    The part I find genuinely useful is what it does to failure. When I catch myself avoiding a hard problem because I might look bad, that's the fixed mindset talking. Reframing it as "I can't do this yet" sounds like a small trick, but it changes what I'm willing to attempt — and the discomfort of being a visible beginner stops feeling like evidence I don't belong.

    I'm trying to bake it into how I work: chasing feedback instead of praise, treating effort as the point rather than proof of inadequacy, and noticing when I'm protecting an image instead of learning. It's less a project and more an ongoing practice — one I'm clearly still working on.
