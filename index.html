What I'm working on

  • 🏭 Throughput — a factory-line optimizer

    A little tool that takes the messy spaghetti of a production line and untangles it into the fastest possible flow. Half serious side project, half excuse to keep playing factory games after work.

    It started as a spreadsheet I kept rebuilding every time I wanted to balance a new build in a factory game. Eventually the spreadsheet got embarrassing enough that I rewrote it as a proper solver: feed it a set of recipes, target outputs, and machine speeds, and it figures out how many of each machine you need, where the bottlenecks are, and which belts will choke first.

    The fun part has been making the output legible. A wall of numbers is useless mid-build, so I'm rendering the whole plan as an interactive graph — nodes for machines, edges sized by throughput — that you can poke at to see what happens when you swap a recipe or overclock a node. It turns out the same constraint-solving that makes a virtual factory hum maps cleanly onto real scheduling problems, which is the part that keeps pulling me back to it.

    Right now I'm cleaning up the solver so it handles loops and byproducts without falling over, and sketching a version that imports save files directly. If you've ever lost a weekend to belt math and have opinions about how this should work, I'd genuinely love to hear them — say hi above.

  • 🥾 Switchbacks — a trail-planning companion

    A planner that turns a vague "let's go hike something this weekend" into a real route — matched to how much elevation my legs actually want and how much daylight is left.

    The idea came from the annoying gap between trail databases and reality. Most apps will happily route you up something with twice the gain you bargained for, or drop you at a trailhead an hour after the good light is gone. I wanted something that reasons about the whole day, not just the line on the map.

    So it pulls elevation profiles, recent trip reports, and a sunrise/sunset window, then ranks routes by how well they fit constraints I set up front — total gain, distance, how exposed I'm willing to be, whether there's a payoff view worth the slog. The output is a simple shortlist with the trade-offs spelled out, instead of an infinite scroll of options.

    It's still rough — the trip-report parsing is held together with optimism — but it's already changed a couple of my weekends for the better. Next up is a packing-and-timing checklist that adapts to the route it picks.

  • 🍳 Mise — notes from every meal worth remembering

    A tiny private journal for tasting menus and home experiments — less a recipe box, more a memory of what made a dish work and how I'd change it next time.

    I take too many food photos and remember almost none of the details that matter — the temperature, the timing, the one substitution that saved the sauce. Mise is my attempt to capture the part of a meal that a photo can't: the reasoning behind it.

    Each entry is fast to log — a few tags, what surprised me, what I'd push further — and over time it's quietly become a map of my own taste. I can pull up everything I've cooked with brown butter, or every dish that leaned on acidity to fix it, and actually see the patterns in what I keep reaching for.

    The dream is to close the loop: let it suggest tweaks for the next attempt based on my own past notes. For now it's just a calm little log that makes me a slightly more deliberate cook, which is enough to keep me adding to it.
